# Body

For a lot of people, the mere mention of showing your finances to another person causes anxiety. Unfortunately, there's no way around it if you're interested in coming to Canada — temporarily as a visitor or student, or permanently through Express Entry's federal economic streams.

## Show money vs. settlement funds — different rules, same idea

"Show money" and "settlement funds" sound similar, but they refer to different application contexts.

### Show money (visitor and study permits)

Proof of funds is how you demonstrate to Canadian immigration that you have enough money to support yourself — and the people travelling with you, in the case of families. If you're applying as a visitor, the calculation is based on the length of your stay and the number of people in your application.

If you're coming as a student, you need to prove you can pay your tuition *and* your living expenses for at least the first year. The official threshold is set by IRCC and is updated periodically — confirm the current amount before applying.

### Settlement funds (Express Entry)

If you're applying under the Federal Skilled Worker Program or the Federal Skilled Trades Program through Express Entry, you must show that you have enough money to settle in Canada once you arrive. The amount scales with your family size — even if your dependants aren't immigrating with you.

The reasoning is straightforward: IRCC wants assurance that if you don't find a job in your first weeks or months, you have the funds to get you through. (Applicants under the Canadian Experience Class — those who've already worked in Canada — are typically exempt from this requirement.)

> The dollar amounts change every year. The principle does not — Canada wants to know you've planned this carefully.

## How much do you actually need?

For visitor and student visa applications, the calculation depends on:

- How many people are in the application (yourself plus accompanying family)
- How long you'll be staying in Canada
- The purpose of the visit (travel or study)

Different application types use different formulas, and submitting the wrong calculation is a common cause of refusal. We provide every client we work with full guidance on these calculations to ensure there are no surprises.

For Express Entry settlement funds, the minimum requirement adjusts every year for inflation and family size. Don't rely on a number you read online last year — pull the current threshold from the official IRCC site or ask a regulated consultant before you submit.

## What documents you'll actually need

You'll need official letters from your bank — usually called bank statements or certifications. They should contain the following:

- Your name and address
- Account numbers (chequing, savings, investment)
- Current balance for each account
- Average balance for the past 6 months
- The bank's letterhead and contact information

Beyond banking documents, other proof of finances can strengthen your file:

- Investment proofs — stock certificates, bonds, retirement accounts
- Real assets — property titles, vehicle registrations, business holdings
- If a parent or relative is funding your travel or studies, their financial documentation is included alongside yours

**RCIC honest take:** Officers look for sudden, unexplained deposits. If you've recently received a large transfer from family, document where it came from — gift letters, transfer receipts, source-of-funds statements — before you submit. A lump sum that "appears" two weeks before you apply is a refusal trigger.
