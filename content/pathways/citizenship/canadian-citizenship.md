# Overview
heading: "What is Canadian Citizenship?"
content: |
  Applying for your Canadian citizenship is the last step of an immigrant's journey. For some it can come a few years after becoming a permanent resident; for others, longer. The good news is that once you qualify, the process is well-defined.

  Permanent resident status is a requirement before you can apply for citizenship. Marrying a Canadian does not make you a citizen on its own — you still need to meet the residency, language, and tax obligations yourself. Permanent residents enjoy nearly all the rights and benefits of citizens, with a few exceptions such as the ability to vote, run for political office, and hold certain high-security federal positions.

  Canadian law allows dual and even multiple citizenships. Becoming Canadian does not, on Canada's side, require you to give up citizenship in another country — but you should confirm with that country's authorities whether they permit dual citizenship.

# FAQ
heading: "Citizenship FAQs"
items:
  - question: "How much does it cost to apply for Canadian citizenship?"
    answer: "For **adults (18 and older)**, the IRCC fees are a **$530 processing fee** plus a **$100 Right of Citizenship fee** — $630 total per adult. For **minors (under 18)**, the processing fee is **$100**. Fees are subject to change; we confirm current amounts during your consultation."
  - question: "What can I do if my citizenship application is refused?"
    answer: "Refusals are usually based on residency, language, or admissibility. Depending on the reason you can re-apply once you meet the requirement, request *judicial review* at the Federal Court within 30 days, or appeal in specific scenarios. We review the refusal letter and recommend the strongest path forward."
  - question: "Do I become a Canadian citizen when I marry a Canadian?"
    answer: "No. Marrying a Canadian does not automatically grant citizenship. You first need to become a permanent resident — typically through [spousal sponsorship](/pages/pathways/family-sponsorship.html) — and then meet the standard residency, language, and tax requirements before applying for citizenship."
  - question: "I'm a citizen of another country. Will I lose that citizenship if I become Canadian?"
    answer: "Canada permits dual and multiple citizenship, so on the Canadian side you do not lose your other citizenship. Whether your country of origin allows you to retain its citizenship after acquiring Canadian citizenship depends on *its* own laws — confirm with that country's authorities before you apply."
