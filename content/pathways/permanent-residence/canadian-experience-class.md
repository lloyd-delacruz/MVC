# Overview
heading: "What is the Canadian Experience Class?"
content: |
  The Canadian Experience Class is one of three federal economic streams
  managed under **Express Entry**. It is designed for people
  who have already worked in a skilled occupation in Canada and want to
  transition to permanent residence. If you came to Canada as a graduate,
  on a work permit, or under an LMIA, CEC is usually the most direct path.

  Because applicants have lived and worked in Canada, the federal
  government considers them lower-risk: language proficiency is established,
  Canadian credentials and work history are documented, and integration is
  already underway. As a result, **CEC has no proof-of-funds
  requirement**, and processing once invited typically targets
  **six months**.

  That said, CEC is competitive. The Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS)
  score that earns an Invitation to Apply (ITA) moves with each draw, and
  small details — language test scores, the timing of your work history,
  spouse points — can be the difference between an invitation and another
  six months in the pool.

# Key Facts
items:
  - "Skilled Work Experience: 1 year (1,560 hours) in Canada, within the last 3 years"
  - "Eligible Occupations: NOC TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3"
  - "Language (TEER 0 or 1): CLB 7 in all four abilities"
  - "Language (TEER 2 or 3): CLB 5 in all four abilities"
  - "Education: Not required (but adds CRS points)"
  - "Proof of Funds: Not required"
  - "Processing After ITA: ~6 months target"

# Do You Qualify
heading: "Do you qualify?"
content: |
  To be eligible for the Canadian Experience Class, you must meet each of
  the following.
items:
  - "At least **1 year (1,560 hours)** of skilled work experience in Canada in the **3 years** before you apply — full-time, part-time equivalent, or combined"
  - "Your work was in a **NOC TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3** occupation — managerial, professional, technical, or skilled trades"
  - "You were **legally authorized to work** in Canada throughout the experience (e.g. PGWP, employer-specific work permit, IEC)"
  - "An approved language test result of **CLB 7** (TEER 0 or 1) or **CLB 5** (TEER 2 or 3) in English or French — speaking, listening, reading, writing"
  - "You plan to live **outside Quebec** (Quebec selects skilled workers under its own programs)"
  - "You are **not inadmissible** on criminal, medical, or security grounds"
note: "What does *not* count: self-employment, work performed while you were a full-time student (including co-op terms), and any work done without authorization. [Book a free CEC eligibility check](/pages/contact.html) — we'll confirm your hours and TEER classification before you spend a dollar on a language test."

# FAQ
heading: "CEC FAQs"
items:
  - question: "Does my Canadian work experience count if I worked while I was a student?"
    answer: "No. Work experience gained while you were a full-time student — including paid co-op work terms — does not count toward the CEC minimum. Only skilled work performed *after* you graduated, on a valid post-graduation work permit or other work authorization, qualifies."
  - question: "I have 18 months of skilled work in Canada — do I qualify?"
    answer: "Likely yes, provided that experience was earned in a NOC TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3 occupation while you were authorized to work, your language test meets CLB 7 (for TEER 0 or 1) or CLB 5 (for TEER 2 or 3), and the experience was within the three years immediately before you apply."
  - question: "Do I need proof of funds for the Canadian Experience Class?"
    answer: "No. CEC applicants are exempt from the settlement funds requirement that applies to the Federal Skilled Worker Program and Federal Skilled Trades Program. You do not need to show bank statements as part of your CEC PR application."
  - question: "Does self-employment in Canada count toward CEC?"
    answer: "No. CEC requires paid work for an employer while you held valid Canadian work authorization. Self-employment income, contract work without an employer relationship, and unpaid internships do not count toward the one-year minimum."
  - question: "My CRS score is below the recent cut-off — am I out?"
    answer: "Not necessarily. CRS cut-offs move every draw, and category-based draws (e.g. French-language proficiency, healthcare, trades) can lower the threshold for qualifying candidates. We review every lever — language retest, additional language, spousal factors, provincial nominee — to find realistic gains."
