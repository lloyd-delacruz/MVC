# Overview
heading: "What is the Federal Skilled Trades Program?"
content: |
  The Federal Skilled Trades Program is the Express Entry stream built specifically for journeypersons and skilled tradespeople. Where the Federal Skilled Worker Program rewards education and language, FSTP rewards hands-on qualification: years on the tools, a certificate of qualification, and an employer who values you enough to put a job offer in writing.

  Canada has a structural shortage of skilled trades, and FSTP reflects that. Language requirements are lower (CLB 5 speaking/listening, CLB 4 reading/writing). Education is not required. The bar is real-world qualification — and that's where most foreign-trained tradespeople hit a wall.

  FSTP requires either a Canadian provincial or territorial certificate of qualification in your trade — typically earned by passing the Red Seal exam — or a valid full-time job offer of at least 1 year from a Canadian employer. Our work is usually making one of those two paths real.

# Do You Qualify

eyebrow: "Eligibility"
lede: "FSTP has four core requirements — meet all of them and you're eligible to enter the Express Entry pool."
items:
  - "2 years (3,120 hours) of paid full-time skilled trades work experience in the last 5 years — Canada or abroad — after you became qualified in your trade"
  - "Work experience in an eligible NOC trade — Major Groups 72 (excluding 726), 73, 82, 83, 92, or 93 (excluding 932)"
  - "Either a Canadian certificate of qualification in your trade, OR a valid full-time job offer of at least 1 year from a Canadian employer"
  - "A language test result of CLB 5 in speaking and listening, and CLB 4 in reading and writing — in English or French"
  - "Settlement funds appropriate for your family size — waived if you have a valid job offer and authorization to work in Canada"
  - "You plan to live outside Quebec and are not inadmissible"
note: "A certificate of qualification almost always means writing the Red Seal exam (or your province's equivalent). If your trade is not regulated in your destination province, you must use the job-offer route. Book a free FSTP eligibility check — we'll map your trade to the right path."

# FAQ
heading: "FSTP FAQs"
eyebrow: "Frequently Asked"
items:
  - question: "Which trades qualify for the Federal Skilled Trades Program?"
    answer: "FSTP eligible occupations are in NOC Major Groups 72 (excluding Sub-Major Group 726), 73, 82, 83, 92, and 93 (excluding Sub-Major Group 932). This covers most industrial, electrical, construction, maintenance, transportation, agricultural, processing, and manufacturing trades — plus chefs, cooks, butchers, and bakers."
  - question: "What language level do I need for FSTP?"
    answer: "FSTP language requirements are lower than other Express Entry streams. You need CLB 5 in speaking and listening, and CLB 4 in reading and writing — in either English or French. This recognizes that trades work depends more on hands-on communication than written reports."
  - question: "Do I need a Canadian certificate of qualification, or will my foreign training do?"
    answer: "Foreign training alone is not enough. You need either a certificate of qualification from a Canadian provincial or territorial trade authority (which usually requires passing the Red Seal exam or equivalent) OR a valid full-time job offer of at least 1 year from a Canadian employer in your trade."
  - question: "How much skilled trades work experience do I need?"
    answer: "You need at least 2 years of full-time skilled trades work experience (or an equivalent amount in part-time work) within the 5 years before you apply — that's 3,120 hours total. The work must be paid and after you became qualified to work in your trade."
  - question: "My province doesn't issue certificates in my trade — what then?"
    answer: "If your destination province doesn't regulate your trade, your only FSTP route is a valid 1-year job offer from a Canadian employer. We can also assess whether a different province (where the trade is regulated) is feasible, or whether the Federal Skilled Worker Program is a better fit."
