What is Canadian Citizenship?

Applying for your Canadian citizenship is the last step of an immigrant's journey. For some it can take as little as three years from the day they became a permanent resident; for others, longer. The good news is that once you qualify, the process is well-defined.

A status of permanent resident is a requirement before you can apply for citizenship. Marrying a Canadian does not make you a citizen on its own — you still need to meet residency, language, and tax obligations. Permanent residents enjoy nearly all the rights and benefits of citizens except the ability to vote, run for political office, and hold certain high-security federal positions.

Canadian law allows dual and even multiple citizenships. Becoming Canadian does not, on Canada's side, force you to give up citizenship in another country — but you should confirm with that country's embassy whether they permit dual citizenship.

Eligibility

Citizenship requirements

To apply for Canadian citizenship, you'll need to meet each of the following.

  • Be a permanent resident of Canada in good standing
  • Have physically lived in Canada for at least 3 of the last 5 years (1,095 days)
  • Have filed your taxes for the relevant years if you were required to
  • Pass the citizenship test on Canada's rights, responsibilities, history, geography, and government (ages 18–54)
  • Demonstrate language ability in English or French at CLB 4 or higher (ages 18–54)

The Process

How it works

From eligibility check to your citizenship oath ceremony.

  1. 1

    Confirm eligibility & physical presence

    We calculate your physical presence days (1,095 minimum), confirm your tax filing history, and review any time you spent outside Canada to make sure you're ready to apply.

  2. 2

    Gather supporting documents

    Travel history, language proof, identity documents, and tax records. We give you a precise checklist and review every item for completeness.

  3. 3

    Submit the citizenship application

    We file your complete application — for you and any eligible family members — to IRCC, with all supporting evidence attached.

  4. 4

    Citizenship test & interview

    If you're between 18 and 54, you'll write the citizenship test (online or in person). We provide study materials and help you prepare for both the test and any interview questions.

  5. 5

    Take the oath

    Once approved, you attend the citizenship ceremony, take the Oath of Citizenship, and receive your citizenship certificate. Welcome, fellow Canadian.

Frequently Asked

Citizenship FAQs

How much does it cost to apply for Canadian citizenship?

For adults (18 and older), the IRCC fees are a $530 processing fee plus a $100 Right of Citizenship fee — $630 total per adult. For minors (under 18), the processing fee is $100. Fees are subject to change; we confirm current amounts during your consultation.

What can I do if my citizenship application is refused?

Refusals are usually based on residency, language, or admissibility. Depending on the reason you can re-apply once you meet the requirement, request judicial review at the Federal Court within 30 days, or appeal in specific scenarios. We review the refusal letter and recommend the strongest path forward.

Do I become a Canadian citizen when I marry a Canadian?

No. Marrying a Canadian does not automatically grant citizenship. You first need to become a permanent resident — typically through spousal sponsorship — and then meet the standard residency, language, and tax requirements before applying for citizenship.

I'm a citizen of another country. Will I lose that citizenship if I become Canadian?

Canada permits dual and multiple citizenship, so on the Canadian side you do not lose your other citizenship. Whether your country of origin allows you to retain its citizenship after acquiring Canadian citizenship depends on its own laws — confirm with that country's embassy before you apply.
